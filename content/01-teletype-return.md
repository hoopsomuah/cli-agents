## A literal machine

In 1963, when you wanted to "use a computer," you walked over to a piece of furniture called a **teletype**. It had three parts that you could touch:

1. A **typewriter-style keyboard** — that's where you typed your commands.
2. A thick **cable** — that ran from the back of the keyboard to a mainframe computer in another room.
3. A **printer** — bolted to the same machine, fed by a continuous roll of paper, that hammered the computer's reply back to you in ink.

That was the user interface. You typed. The machine clacked. The paper rolled. The conversation between human and computer was made of **characters going in one direction and characters coming back in the other**. There were no windows, no mouse, no graphics. Just text on paper.

## What changed, and what didn't

In the 1970s and 1980s, three things were swapped out:

- The **paper roll** was replaced by a **glass CRT screen**. Same characters, less litter.
- The **mainframe in another room** was replaced by a **computer on the desk**.
- The **physical cable** was replaced by an in-memory software pipe called a **pseudo-teletype**, or **PTY**.

What did *not* change is the contract: **characters in, characters back**. The black window on your laptop today — Windows Terminal, macOS Terminal, iTerm — is a software emulation of that 1963 piece of furniture. It is, quite literally, an electronic ghost of a printer.

## Why we are starting here

Because the next two hours of your week will involve typing characters into a black window and reading characters back. If you carry the mental picture of a typewriter wired to a computer wired to a printer, the rest of the terminal will stop being mysterious. It is the most durable user interface in computing — and now AI agents speak it fluently too.
